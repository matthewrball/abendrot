## ⚠️ Read this first — the four launch-critical guardrails

These come from the adversarial critic and are **binding** (§13 + FTC). Breaking them is the
fastest way to turn a wellness app into an unsubstantiated-health-claims problem.

1. **Couple warmth with brightness.** The app warms but does NOT dim, and the circadian "dose"
   is driven by intensity (Zeitzer 2000). A bright white screen at 1900K can still exceed the
   ≤10-lux melanopic-EDI evening target. Any claim about circadian benefit must pair "removes blue"
   with **"and lower your brightness"** — otherwise it overstates what the engine alone does.
2. **Red is a circadian-SPARING footnote, never a therapy hero.** The brand name *Abendrot*
   ("sunset red") + red imagery already creates *implied* red-light-therapy risk. FTC reads net
   impression, not just words (Willow Curve, $22M). Keep red as "low-melanopic / circadian-sparing",
   never photobiomodulation. **Imagery guardrail, not just wording.**
3. **Never juxtapose the product sentence with sleep-latency data.** Pairing "Abendrot does X" next
   to melatonin/sleep-onset findings implies a sleep benefit our own cited RCT (Duraccio 2021) failed
   to find. Keep the product claim and the science citation in separate breaths.
4. **Precise spectral/CCT numbers are standard-curve illustrations, not measured product output.**
   Never say "Abendrot reduces your melanopic exposure by X%". The s_mel weights and the sub-1900K
   figures are model-derived; label them as illustrative.

## Approved headline claims

Each is rated by evidence strength. Use the **§13-safe wording** verbatim or paraphrase carefully —
do not strengthen the verbs.

### 1. The light wavelengths that matter most for the body clock at night are in the blue (~459-490 nm). That is exactly the band Abendrot's warming removes first: blue output reaches zero at ~1900K.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> Research into the body's circadian system has identified short-wavelength blue light (peak sensitivity around 459-490 nm) as the dominant input for nighttime melatonin and circadian regulation [Brainard 2001; Thapan 2001; CIE S 026:2018]. Abendrot's warming attenuates the display's blue primary, reaching zero blue output at approximately 1900K.

- **Exact levels:** Melatonin-suppression action-spectrum peak: 464 nm (Brainard 2001, opsin-template fit R^2=0.91) and 459 nm (Thapan 2001, r^2=0.74). Most potent region 446-477 nm. Standardized melanopic (ipRGC) sensitivity peaks ~490 nm in vivo (CIE S 026:2018). Abendrot blue gain = 0 at ~1900K.
- **Cite:** Brainard et al. 2001, J Neurosci, DOI 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.21-16-06405.2001; Thapan et al. 2001, J Physiol, DOI 10.1111/j.1469-7793.2001.t01-1-00261.x; CIE S 026:2018, DOI 10.25039/S026.2018

### 2. It is the melanopic (short-wavelength) CONTENT of evening screen light, not the brightness or color appearance, that drives the effect on melatonin and sleep onset.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> In a controlled human display study, the melanopic (short-wavelength) content of evening screen light - independent of luminance and color appearance - was shown to determine its effect on melatonin and time to fall asleep [Schoellhorn et al. 2023]. Abendrot is designed to lower a screen's melanopic content by attenuating the blue primary.

- **Exact levels:** n=72 healthy males (18-35y); exposure ~4 h before habitual bedtime; melanopic EDI manipulated independent of luminance/color via 5-primary metameric display (~200-300% melanopsin Weber contrast). Low-melanopic (~5 lx mEDI) vs high-melanopic (~150 lx mEDI): ~50% shorter mean sleep latency and attenuated melatonin suppression under low-melanopic.
- **Cite:** Schoellhorn et al. 2023, Communications Biology, DOI 10.1038/s42003-023-04598-4; Gimenez et al. 2022, J Pineal Res, DOI 10.1111/jpi.12786

### 3. Long-wavelength red light is circadian-sparing: at practical evening intensities it produces minimal-to-no melatonin suppression, while matched blue light suppresses melatonin.
**Evidence: MODERATE**

> Research shows that long-wavelength (red) light is circadian-sparing - it produces little to no nocturnal melatonin suppression at practical evening intensities, whereas matched blue light does [Figueiro & Rea 2010; Sanchez-Cano et al. 2025]. This is a low-melanopic-stimulation property and is distinct from any 'red-light therapy' health claim.

- **Exact levels:** Figueiro & Rea 2010: red 625 nm vs blue 470 nm, both 40 lux at the cornea, 1 h at night; only blue significantly suppressed melatonin (blue vs dark P=.006, blue vs red P=.004); red was statistically indistinguishable from darkness. Sanchez-Cano 2025: red 631 nm vs blue 464 nm, 3 h (21:00-24:00), 80 lux photopic, n=12; at 2 h melatonin 26.0 pg/mL (red) vs 7.5 pg/mL (blue), p=0.019; at 3 h 16.6 vs 8.3 pg/mL, p=0.013.
- **Cite:** Figueiro & Rea 2010, Int J Endocrinol, DOI 10.1155/2010/829351; Sanchez-Cano et al. 2025, Life (MDPI), DOI 10.3390/life15050715

### 4. Eliminating blue (the ~1900K point) removes the dominant circadian stimulus; per the CIE melanopic curve, the residual green region carries far less weight and red is essentially off the active range.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> Per the official CIE S 026:2018 melanopic action spectrum, sensitivity falls steeply beyond green and is near-zero in the red. Once the blue primary is removed (Abendrot reaches zero blue at ~1900K), the dominant melanopic stimulus is already eliminated; the green region carries a much smaller weight and red is effectively outside the active band.

- **Exact levels:** CIE S 026:2018 melanopic relative sensitivity (peak=1.0 at 490 nm): 460nm=0.708, 480nm=0.966, 490nm=1.000, 500nm=0.966, 530nm=0.519, 550nm=0.216, 555nm=0.162, 580nm=0.0269, 600nm=0.00507 (~0.5% of peak), 620nm=0.000947 (~0.1%), 650nm=0.0000866 (~0.009%). sRGB green primary (~550 nm) carries ~0.22 weight; red primary (~610 nm) ~0.002.
- **Cite:** CIE S 026:2018, DOI 10.25039/S026.2018 (official data file S026_Table2_Data.xlsx); Schlangen & Price 2021, Front Neurol, DOI 10.3389/fneur.2021.624861

### 5. Expert consensus gives concrete evening/night light targets in melanopic EDI: <=10 lux in the evening (>=3 h before bed) and <=1 lux during sleep, vs >=250 lux in the daytime.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> An 18-author international expert consensus recommends keeping evening melanopic EDI at or below 10 lux (starting at least 3 hours before bed) and the sleep environment below 1 lux, versus a daytime minimum of 250 lux [Brown et al. 2022]. These are healthy-adult reference targets - not medical thresholds. Abendrot helps users lower the melanopic content of on-screen light toward these reference levels.

- **Exact levels:** Daytime minimum 250 lux melanopic EDI; evening maximum 10 lux melanopic EDI (starting >=3 h before bedtime); sleep environment maximum 1 lux melanopic EDI (10 lux if vision is needed at night). All measured at the eye, vertical plane, ~1.2 m height. mEDI defined per CIE S 026:2018 (K_mel,v(D65) = 1.3262 mW/lm).
- **Cite:** Brown et al. 2022, PLoS Biology, DOI 10.1371/journal.pbio.3001571; CIE S 026:2018, DOI 10.25039/S026.2018

### 6. Correlated color temperature (Kelvin) is NOT a reliable proxy for circadian potency; at one fixed CCT melanopic EDI can vary roughly two-fold by spectral composition - so 'lower Kelvin = more protective' must not be stated as a guarantee.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> Research shows correlated color temperature (CCT) is not a suitable proxy for the biological potency of light: at a fixed CCT, melanopic EDI can vary roughly two-fold depending on spectral composition [Esposito & Houser 2022]. The relevant quantity is melanopic content (where blue is removed), not the Kelvin label itself.

- **Exact levels:** At a fixed 5000 K (300 lx photopic, Rf>=70), melanopic EDI varied from 196 to 319 lx (a 123 lx spread). Across 2500-6000 K, achievable melanopic EDI ranged 123-354 lx at matched illuminance/fidelity.
- **Cite:** Esposito & Houser 2022, Scientific Reports, DOI 10.1038/s41598-022-21755-7

### 7. Individual sensitivity to evening light varies more than 50-fold, so no single warmth/brightness setting is right for everyone - which is why Abendrot offers an adjustable slider and uses humble, non-guarantee language.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> Human sensitivity to evening light varies more than 50-fold between individuals [Phillips et al. 2019], so no single setting is optimal for everyone. Abendrot provides a user-adjustable warmth slider and frames its effect as variable individual support, never a guaranteed outcome.

- **Exact levels:** Group-level ED50 for melatonin suppression = 24.6 lux; individual ED50 ranged 6 lux to 350 lux (>50-fold). 50% melatonin suppression occurred at <30 lux at the group level (comparable to typical indoor/device light). n=55, ages 18-30.
- **Cite:** Phillips et al. 2019, PNAS, DOI 10.1073/pnas.1901824116

### 8. Most of the circadian benefit comes from leaving the bright/blue-rich zone (the steep part of the dose-response), since even dim-to-moderate light suppresses much melatonin and the response saturates near ~200 lux.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> Human melatonin suppression follows a steep, saturating dose-response to light: half-maximal suppression occurs at only ~50-130 lux and the response approaches saturation near ~200 lux [Zeitzer et al. 2000]. The largest reductions therefore come from getting out of the bright/blue-rich zone rather than from chasing the last few percent toward pure red.

- **Exact levels:** Half-maximal melatonin suppression at ~50-130 lux (4-parameter logistic ED50 = 106 +/- 13.3 lux); near-saturation by ~200 lux; phase-resetting half-maximal ~80-160 lux, saturating ~550 lux; ~1% of 9000 lux bright light (~100 lux room light) yields half the maximal response. n=23, 6.5 h nocturnal exposure.
- **Cite:** Zeitzer et al. 2000, J Physiol, DOI 10.1111/j.1469-7793.2000.00695.x

### 9. Green's circadian weight is real but largely transient: cones (including the ~550 nm green region) contribute mainly in the first 1-3 hours of an exposure, after which melanopsin (blue, ~481 nm) dominates over a sustained evening.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> The circadian action spectrum is duration-dependent: over a sustained multi-hour evening exposure, melanopsin in the blue (~481-485 nm) dominates, with the green-region cone contribution being strong mainly early and then collapsing [St Hilaire et al. 2022; Gooley et al. 2010]. Over a full evening the long-duration blue channel - which Abendrot zeroes by ~1900K - is the dominant driver.

- **Exact levels:** St Hilaire 2022 (6.5 h): whole-exposure melatonin-suppression lambda-max = 481 nm (lens-corrected), phase-resetting 483 nm; first quarter peak 441 nm with secondary 550 nm (S-cone 51%, L+M-cone 47%); final quarter 485 nm. Three-channel split of full response: ipRGC/melanopsin 81%, S-cone 12%, L+M-cone 7%. Gooley 2010: 555 nm green sensitivity decayed relative to 460 nm with a half-life of ~38 min.
- **Cite:** St Hilaire et al. 2022, PNAS, DOI 10.1073/pnas.2205301119; Gooley et al. 2010, Sci Transl Med, DOI 10.1126/scitranslmed.3000741

### 10. Removing blue at the source (the protective variable) - not the color 'red' per se - is what blocks light-induced melatonin suppression, even under bright light.
**Evidence: MODERATE**

> Blocking the blue band alone is sufficient to prevent light-induced melatonin suppression in humans, even under bright light, establishing that the protective variable is the removal of short-wavelength content rather than the color red itself [Sasseville et al. 2006].

- **Exact levels:** 60-min bright-light pulse, 1300 lux behind filters, 01:00-02:00. Orange (blue-blocking) lens: non-significant +6% melatonin change (no suppression); grey control lens: significant -46% melatonin suppression (P<0.05). n=14.
- **Cite:** Sasseville et al. 2006, J Pineal Res, DOI 10.1111/j.1600-079X.2006.00332.x

### 11. Software blue-reduction modes have not shown a measurable sleep benefit in randomized real-world testing, so the honest claim is 'reduces blue-light exposure at night', not 'improves sleep'.
**Evidence: MODERATE**

> A randomized real-world study found no measurable sleep-outcome difference from a software blue-reduction mode (Apple Night Shift) versus no reduction [Duraccio et al. 2021]. Accordingly, Abendrot describes what it demonstrably does - reduces blue-light exposure at night - rather than promising a sleep improvement.

- **Exact levels:** Randomized, n=167 emerging adults (18-24, 71.3% female), 7 consecutive nights, wrist accelerometry: no significant difference in sleep outcomes between iPhone-with-Night-Shift, iPhone-without-Night-Shift, and no-phone groups (the full-sample result was null).
- **Cite:** Duraccio, Zaugg, Blackburn, Jensen 2021, Sleep Health, DOI 10.1016/j.sleh.2021.03.005

### 12. ipRGCs are a distinct third class of retinal photoreceptor that set the circadian clock, and their melanopsin pigment is maximally sensitive to blue light (~480-484 nm) - the physiological reason blue is the circadian lever.
**Evidence: STRONG**

> Melanopsin-containing ipRGCs are a distinct class of retinal photoreceptor that drive the circadian clock, with intrinsic peak sensitivity in the blue at ~480-484 nm [Berson et al. 2002; Provencio et al. 2000; Bailes & Lucas 2013]. This is the physiological basis for blue being the key circadian wavelength.

- **Exact levels:** Intrinsic ipRGC action-spectrum lambda-max = 484 nm (Berson 2002); human melanopsin photopigment lambda-max ~479 nm (Bailes & Lucas 2013). Human OPN4 gene: 10 exons, chromosome 10q22, 2.3-kb cDNA; expression restricted to inner-retinal ganglion/amacrine cells, absent from rod/cone photoreceptors (Provencio 2000).
- **Cite:** Berson, Dunn & Takao 2002, Science, DOI 10.1126/science.1067262; Provencio et al. 2000, J Neurosci, DOI 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.20-02-00600.2000; Bailes & Lucas 2013, Proc R Soc B, DOI 10.1098/rspb.2012.2987

## The red-light story (approved narrative)

Use this as the canonical framing whenever red/warm light comes up. It is a **circadian-sparing**
story, not a therapy story.

Abendrot's red/warm evening story is a CIRCADIAN-SPARING story, not a therapy story. The science is about what red light does NOT do to the body clock.

The body's circadian system is driven mainly by a dedicated blue-light sensor. Melanopsin-containing ipRGCs - a distinct third class of retinal photoreceptor that sets the circadian clock (Berson 2002; Provencio 2000) - are maximally sensitive to blue light around 480 nm (Bailes & Lucas 2013, human melanopsin lambda-max ~479 nm). The two foundational human action spectra for melatonin suppression both land in the blue: 464 nm (Brainard 2001) and 459 nm (Thapan 2001), with the most potent region being 446-477 nm. The standardized CIE S 026:2018 melanopic curve peaks at 490 nm and falls steeply toward longer wavelengths - by 600 nm it is ~0.5% of peak, by 620 nm ~0.1%.

Because long-wavelength red light sits on the far, near-zero tail of that curve, it is circadian-sparing: it stimulates the melanopic system very little. This is shown directly in humans. Figueiro & Rea (2010) exposed people to red (625 nm) and blue (470 nm) at the SAME 40 lux at the eye for an hour at night - only blue suppressed melatonin; red was statistically indistinguishable from darkness. Sanchez-Cano et al. (2025) compared red 631 nm vs blue 464 nm over a 3-hour evening exposure: under red, melatonin recovered to 26.0 pg/mL while blue held it suppressed at 7.5 pg/mL (p=0.019). And the protective variable is the short-wavelength removal itself, not the color red: blocking just the blue band with orange lenses prevented melatonin suppression under bright light, while neutral-grey lenses did not (Sasseville 2006).

Two honesty guardrails are built into this story. First, circadian-sparing is RELATIVE, not absolute: at extreme irradiance, narrow-band red (631 nm at ~13 log photons/cm^2/s) still produced cone-mediated circadian phase shifts in some individuals even without melatonin suppression (Ho Mien/Gooley 2014). So we say 'low-stimulus / circadian-sparing', never 'zero circadian effect'. Second, and most important: this is the low-melanopic-stimulation claim ONLY. It is categorically different from 'red-light therapy' / photobiomodulation health claims (treating pain, healing tissue, 'clinically proven'), which are a separate, FTC-enforced, far weaker evidence base - the FTC won a $22M judgment against one such device for unsupported 'clinically proven' claims (FTC 2020). Abendrot must never conflate the two. Our claim is simply: warm/red evening light puts far less energy in the blue band the body clock is tuned to detect.

Engineering footnote we can stand behind: once Abendrot's warming zeroes the blue primary (~1900K), the dominant melanopic stimulus is already removed; pushing further toward pure red removes only the residual green channel, a real but small additional melanopic reduction (green ~0.22 vs blue's much larger weight on the CIE curve), while legibility degrades - which is why mainstream warming tools like f.lux set their normal floor at ~1900K and gate redder values behind an opt-in advanced range.

## ⛔ DO NOT CLAIM (the banned list)

- Do NOT make red-light-therapy / photobiomodulation health claims (treats pain, heals tissue, reduces inflammation, boosts recovery/cognition). Abendrot's claim is ONLY 'red/warm light is circadian-sparing / low-melanopic'. These are categorically different evidence bases; conflating them is the single biggest §13/FTC risk (FTC won a $22M judgment over 'clinically proven' LLLT pain claims).
- Do NOT say 'clinically proven', 'proven to improve sleep', 'cures insomnia', or 'treats insomnia'. Abendrot is a general-wellness app, not a medical device. Software blue-reduction has NOT shown measurable sleep benefit in randomized testing (Duraccio 2021).
- Do NOT guarantee 'improves sleep' or 'you will fall asleep faster' as a promised outcome. The honest claim is 'reduces blue-light exposure at night' / 'supports healthy evening light habits' - an input the engine demonstrably changes, not a guaranteed downstream result.
- Do NOT say 'blue light damages your eyes' or imply retinal/eye-damage protection. The evidence base here is circadian (melatonin/sleep timing), not ocular damage; that is a different and unsupported claim.
- Do NOT claim 'cures eye strain' or 'prevents eye strain'. Not supported by the cited circadian literature.
- Do NOT assert 'lower Kelvin = more circadian-protective' as a guarantee. CCT is not a valid circadian proxy - melanopic EDI varies ~2-fold at a fixed CCT (Esposito & Houser 2022). Speak in melanopic terms.
- Do NOT claim red light has 'zero circadian effect' or is 'circadian-inert'. It is circadian-SPARING / low-stimulus and this is RELATIVE and intensity-dependent - high-irradiance red still produced cone-mediated phase shifts in some people (Ho Mien/Gooley 2014).
- Do NOT claim pure red is necessary or universally optimal. Individual sensitivity varies >50-fold (Phillips 2019); the consensus evening target (<=10 lux mEDI, Brown 2022) is reached well before pure red, and green's circadian weight is largely transient (St Hilaire 2022).
- Do NOT cite the Sanchez-Cano 2025 red-vs-blue pg/mL values (26.0 vs 7.5) as coming from Ho Mien/Gooley 2014 - that PLOS ONE study has NO blue arm and reports % AUC, not pg/mL. Keep the two sources separate.
- Do NOT overstate Brainard 2001 fit quality as 'r=0.81-0.99'; the correct fluence-response range is r=0.81-0.95.
- Do NOT present the derived display M/P figures (e.g., '77/23 split', 'M/P 26% at 1900K', '4% at pure red') as established/peer-reviewed; they are self-derived model estimates with wide ranges - present only as illustrative engineering modeling, not as cited fact.
- Do NOT attribute the f.lux '1900K floor / no biological reason to go lower' rationale to f.lux's makers; it is unofficial forum (community user) opinion. Cite only the verifiable product behavior (1900K default floor, opt-in wider range) to the official f.lux FAQ.
- Do NOT cite the Duraccio 2021 Night Shift study as evidence for the 1900K/melanopic-threshold claim - it tested only Night Shift on/off/no-phone and says nothing about CCT targets or melanopic EDI.
- Do NOT present Brown et al. 2022 consensus numbers as regulatory or medical limits - they are healthy-adult, group-average reference targets with explicit individual-variability caveats; frame as 'reference targets', never promised outcomes.
- Do NOT misattribute the melanopic ~490 nm peak / mEDI definition to 'Spitschan et al.' for PMC7970181 - the correct authors are Schlangen & Price 2021 (Front Neurol, DOI 10.3389/fneur.2021.624861).

## Citation library (24 verified papers)

Every paper below was confirmed real + peer-reviewed and checked to actually support its claim.
Link these (DOIs) rather than asserting outcomes. Ideal for the website "The Science" panel, SEO
reference sections, and AEO answer-grounding.

- **Brainard GC, Hanifin JP, Greeson JM, Byrne B, Glickman G, Gerner E, Rollag MD** (2001). *Action Spectrum for Melatonin Regulation in Humans: Evidence for a Novel Circadian Photoreceptor* — The Journal of Neuroscience. `10.1523/JNEUROSCI.21-16-06405.2001`

  - Official standardized melanopic action spectrum s_mel(lambda) (peak 490 nm) and melanopic EDI metric (K_mel,v(D65)=1.3262 mW/lm). Verified relative sensitivities: 600nm=0.51%, 620nm=0.095%, 650nm=0.0087% of peak; green (~550 nm)=0.216, red (~610 nm)~0.002.
- **Schoellhorn I, Stefani O, Lucas RJ, Spitschan M, Slawik HC, Cajochen C** (2023). *Melanopic irradiance defines the impact of evening display light on sleep latency, melatonin and alertness* — Communications Biology (Nature Portfolio). `10.1038/s42003-023-04598-4`
  - Strongest direct display evidence: melanopic content (not luminance/color) drives evening melatonin, sleep latency and alertness. n=72 males; low-melanopic (~5 lx mEDI) vs high (~150 lx mEDI) gave ~50% shorter sleep latency and attenuated melatonin suppression. (Alertness was not a clean dose-response.)
- **Sanchez-Cano A, Luesma-Bartolome MJ, Solanas E, Orduna-Hospital E** (2025). *Comparative Effects of Red and Blue LED Light on Melatonin Levels During Three-Hour Exposure in Healthy Adults* — Life (Basel, MDPI). `10.3390/life15050715`
  - Within-subject crossover (n=12): red 631 nm vs blue 464 nm, 3 h evening, 80 lux photopic. At 2 h melatonin 26.0 pg/mL (red) vs 7.5 pg/mL (blue), p=0.019; at 3 h 16.6 vs 8.3, p=0.013. Red 'less disruptive', not circadian-neutral. Small pilot.
- **Figueiro MG, Rea MS** (2010). *The Effects of Red and Blue Lights on Circadian Variations in Cortisol, Alpha Amylase, and Melatonin* — International Journal of Endocrinology. `10.1155/2010/829351`
  - Cleanest matched-illuminance demonstration: red 625 nm vs blue 470 nm, both 40 lux at the cornea, 1 h at night; only blue suppressed melatonin (blue vs dark P=.006, blue vs red P=.004); red was statistically indistinguishable from darkness. n=12.
- **Sasseville A, Paquet N, Sevigny J, Hebert M** (2006). *Blue blocker glasses impede the capacity of bright light to suppress melatonin production* — Journal of Pineal Research. `10.1111/j.1600-079X.2006.00332.x`
  - Blocking the blue band alone prevented melatonin suppression under bright light: orange (blue-blocking) lens +6% (non-significant) vs grey control -46% (P<0.05), 1300 lux pulse 01:00-02:00. Protective variable = short-wavelength removal, not color red. n=14.
- **Phillips AJK, Vidafar P, Burns AC, McGlashan EM, Anderson C, Rajaratnam SMW, Lockley SW, Cain SW** (2019). *High sensitivity and interindividual variability in the response of the human circadian system to evening light* — Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS). `10.1073/pnas.1901824116`
  - >50-fold interindividual variability in evening-light sensitivity: group ED50 = 24.6 lux, individual ED50s 6-350 lux; 50% melatonin suppression at <30 lux (typical indoor/device level). Basis for adjustable slider + humble framing. n=55.
- **Zeitzer JM, Dijk DJ, Kronauer RE, Brown EN, Czeisler CA** (2000). *Sensitivity of the human circadian pacemaker to nocturnal light: melatonin phase resetting and suppression* — The Journal of Physiology. `10.1111/j.1469-7793.2000.00695.x`
  - Steep, saturating dose-response: half-maximal melatonin suppression at ~50-130 lux (ED50=106+/-13.3 lux), near-saturation ~200 lux; ~1% of 9000 lux (~100 lux room light) gives half-maximal response. Biggest gains come from leaving the bright zone. n=23, 6.5 h.
- **St Hilaire MA, Amundadottir ML, Rahman SA, Rajaratnam SMW, Ruger M, Brainard GC, Czeisler CA, Andersen M, Gooley JJ, Lockley SW** (2022). *The spectral sensitivity of human circadian phase resetting and melatonin suppression to light changes dynamically with light duration* — Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS). `10.1073/pnas.2205301119`
  - Action spectrum is duration-dependent: first quarter peaks 441 nm with secondary 550 nm (cones), final quarter 485 nm (melanopsin). Sustained 6.5 h melatonin-suppression lambda-max=481 nm; three-channel split ipRGC 81% / S-cone 12% / L+M-cone 7%. Green's weight is transient.
- **Gooley JJ, Rajaratnam SMW, Brainard GC, Kronauer RE, Czeisler CA, Lockley SW** (2010). *Spectral responses of the human circadian system depend on the irradiance and duration of exposure to light* — Science Translational Medicine. `10.1126/scitranslmed.3000741`
  - At the start of exposure, 555 nm green was as effective as 460 nm blue at suppressing melatonin (cone contribution), but green sensitivity decayed exponentially with a ~38 min half-life while blue (melanopsin) sustained suppression. Green is transient, not sustained.
- **Esposito T, Houser K** (2022). *Correlated color temperature is not a suitable proxy for the biological potency of light* — Scientific Reports (Nature Portfolio). `10.1038/s41598-022-21755-7`
  - CCT is not a valid circadian proxy: melanopic EDI varied 123-354 lx across 2500-6000 K and up to 123 lx at a single fixed CCT (196-319 lx at 5000 K) purely from spectral composition. Speak in melanopic terms, not Kelvin guarantees.
- **Berson DM, Dunn FA, Takao M** (2002). *Phototransduction by retinal ganglion cells that set the circadian clock* — Science. `10.1126/science.1067262`
  - ipRGCs are a third class of photoreceptor that depolarize to light even with all rod/cone input blocked; intrinsic action-spectrum lambda-max = 484 nm (vitamin-A opsin). Physiological anchor for blue ~480 nm as the key circadian wavelength.
- **Provencio I, Rodriguez IR, Jiang G, Hayes WP, Moreira EF, Rollag MD** (2000). *A Novel Human Opsin in the Inner Retina* — The Journal of Neuroscience. `10.1523/JNEUROSCI.20-02-00600.2000`
  - Identified/localized human melanopsin (OPN4): 10 exons, chromosome 10q22, 2.3-kb cDNA; expression restricted to inner-retinal ganglion/amacrine cells, absent from rod/cone photoreceptors. (Cite for localization, not spectral tuning.)
- **Bailes HJ, Lucas RJ** (2013). *Human melanopsin forms a pigment maximally sensitive to blue light (lambda-max approximately 479 nm)* — Proceedings of the Royal Society B. `10.1098/rspb.2012.2987`
  - Measured human melanopsin photopigment lambda-max ~479 nm - maximally sensitive to blue light. Corroborates the melanopic peak and the blue-as-circadian-lever physiology.
- **Gimenez MC, Stefani O, Cajochen C, Lang D, Deuring G, Schlangen LJM** (2022). *Predicting melatonin suppression by light in humans: Unifying photoreceptor-based equivalent daylight illuminances, spectral composition, timing and duration of light exposure* — Journal of Pineal Research. `10.1111/jpi.12786`
  - Pooled analysis of 29 human studies: melatonin suppression dominated by (1) melanopic EDI, (2) exposure duration, (3) pupil dilation, (4) S-cone-opic EDI. Melanopic EDI was the best single predictor (except <21 lux where S-cone-opic edged it). Confirms melanopic content + duration over photopic brightness.
- **Chang AM, Aeschbach D, Duffy JF, Czeisler CA** (2015). *Evening use of light-emitting eReaders negatively affects sleep, circadian timing, and next-morning alertness* — Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS). `10.1073/pnas.1418490112`
  - Real-device evening reading on a blue-enriched iPad (~31 lux at eye, peak ~450 nm) suppressed evening melatonin by 55.12% (vs none with print) and delayed melatonin onset >1.5 h. Establishes that dim-but-blue-enriched display light has a real circadian effect. n=12, 5 nights.
- **Duraccio KM, Zaugg KK, Blackburn RC, Jensen CD** (2021). *Does iPhone Night Shift mitigate negative effects of smartphone use on sleep outcomes in emerging adults?* — Sleep Health (Journal of the National Sleep Foundation). `10.1016/j.sleh.2021.03.005`
  - Randomized real-world RCT (n=167, 7 nights, actigraphy): NO significant sleep-outcome difference between Night-Shift-on, Night-Shift-off, and no-phone groups (full-sample null). Software blue-reduction did not demonstrably improve sleep. (Original dossier byline 'Jones et al.' was fabricated; corrected here.)
- **Spitschan M, Jain S, Brainard DH, Aguirre GK** (2014). *Opponent melanopsin and S-cone signals in the human pupillary light response* — Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS). `10.1073/pnas.1400942111`
  - Non-visual circuits show spectral opponency: melanopsin signals add with L+M cones but are opposed by S cones in pupil control, so short-wavelength light is the salient axis these circuits detect. (Primate retinal-circuit mechanism additionally per Dacey et al. 2005.)
- **al Enezi J, Revell V, Brown T, Wynne J, Schlangen L, Lucas RJ** (2011). *A 'melanopic' spectral efficiency function predicts the sensitivity of melanopsin photoreceptors to polychromatic lights* — Journal of Biological Rhythms. `10.1177/0748730411409719`
  - Defines the melanopic weighting function (melanopsin lambda-max ~480 nm) underlying CIE S 026 - the basis for treating red (~630 nm) as far off-peak/circadian-sparing. (Do NOT cite this paper for a 470-vs-635 nm human PLR contrast; that belongs to Gamlin 2007 / McDougal & Gamlin 2010.)
- **U.S. Federal Trade Commission** (2020). *FTC Puts an End to Deceptive Advertising of Light Therapy Device (Willow Curve)* — FTC.gov news release (government enforcement, not peer-reviewed). `https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2020/06/ftc-puts-end-deceptive-advertising-light-therapy-device`
  - FTC obtained a $22M judgment (partially suspended after owners each paid $200,000) against a low-level-light-therapy device marketed as 'clinically proven' to treat pain without evidence. Defines red-light/photobiomodulation HEALTH claims as a distinct, FTC-enforced category Abendrot must not enter.

## Honest limitations to keep visible (builds trust, reduces FTC risk)

- THE RED-LIGHT STORY IS THE SHAKIEST LINK BY FAR, because it is the most marketing-attractive AND rests on the engine's least-defensible region. The memo itself concedes (uncertainties #1, #2) that the sub-1900K / pure-red endpoint is a MODEL EXTRAPOLATION below Tanner-Helland's valid floor (~1000K), and that the 'green step is the minority' split is author-derived, not literature-stated, with wide ranges (blue 60-77% / green 23-40%). Yet the marketing redLightStory and spectrumTable present a confident '~505K = pure red' endpoint and precise s_mel weights as if grounded. I verified gain(505K) does reach G=0.000 in code, but that is a model artifact, not a validated display state. If marketing leans into 'red/warm' as the hero, it is anchoring on the weakest-evidenced part of the entire analysis.
- 'CIRCADIAN-SPARING' IS RELATIVE AND INTENSITY-DEPENDENT, AND THE MEMO KNOWS IT (uncertainty #3, Ho Mien 2014: high-irradiance 631nm red still caused cone-mediated phase shifts with no melatonin suppression). The marketing wording is mostly careful ('low-melanopic-stimulation', 'circadian-sparing, never zero'), but the redLightStory narrative momentum ('only blue suppressed melatonin; red was statistically indistinguishable from darkness') invites a reader to hear 'red = safe/inert'. The guardrail is one sentence against several persuasive sentences. Imbalanced.
- THE SANCHEZ-CANO 2025 EVIDENCE IS A WEAK PILLAR DOING VISIBLE WORK. It is n=12, a small pilot (the citationLibrary admits 'small pilot'), in MDPI's Life, and its own framing is 'red less disruptive', not circadian-neutral. It is used in a 'moderate' strength claim, which is honest, but it is one of only two supports for the long-wavelength-red claim and the other (Figueiro 2010) is also n=12 and 1 hour. The red claim's entire human-evidence base is two small short studies.
- THE CONSENSUS-TARGET CLAIM (Brown 2022 <=10 lux mEDI) IS STRONG SCIENCE BUT WEAKLY CONNECTED TO THE PRODUCT. Stating the target is fine; the weak link is the unstated implication that Abendrot reaches it. With no luminance control and no mEDI measurement, the app cannot demonstrate it crosses the <=10 lux line. Pairing a hard consensus number with a product that cannot verify compliance is an inference trap.
- SELF-DERIVED DISPLAY M/P FIGURES. The memo's headline quantitative split (26-39% mel output at 1900K, 77/23 blue-vs-green) could not be reproduced even by the memo's own audit (uncertainty #1). The doNotClaim list correctly forbids presenting these as established - but the spectrumTable still publishes precise s_mel-times-primary weights (blue 0.785, green 0.228, red 0.002) that a designer will naturally turn into exactly the forbidden percentage graphic. The firewall between 'illustrative' and 'cited' is procedurally thin.
