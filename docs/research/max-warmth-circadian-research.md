## TL;DR — Engineering decision

**Recommendation: Option HYBRID** — floor & default warmest point at **1900K** (down from the current 500K), default strength **0.7**, pure-red only behind an explicit opt-in "expanded range" control (or dropped entirely → pure Option A).

- `Kelvin.warmestSupported = 1900`
- `EngineConfiguration.defaultWarmestPoint = Kelvin(1900)`
- default strength = 0.7

### Q1 — Does removing GREEN below ~1900K add meaningful circadian benefit?

NEGLIGIBLE in absolute terms; a large RATIO cut of an already-tiny residual. By ~1900K the Tanner-Helland engine has zeroed the blue channel (confirmed in code: blackbodyWhite sets blue=0 at temp<=19, i.e. <=1900K), so the dominant circadian stimulus is already gone. The melatonin/melanopic action spectra peak in the blue (Brainard 2001 lambda-max 464nm; Thapan 2001 459nm; Berson 2002 ipRGC 484nm; CIE S 026:2018 melanopic peak 490nm). On the official CIE s_mel curve the residual at the green/red primaries is small: s_mel(550nm)~0.216, s_mel(555nm)~0.162, s_mel(610nm)~0.0022 (~1/460 of peak), s_mel(620nm)~0.0009. So once blue is removed, essentially ALL remaining melanopic output is carried by the sRGB GREEN primary (~550nm) and the red primary is melanopically near-inert. Pushing below 1900K toward pure red (~505K, green gain->0) removes that last green-borne residual: a derived primary-scaling model puts melanopic output at roughly 26-39% of the 6500K baseline at 1900K (green-dominated) falling to ~0.2-4% at pure red. Framed as fraction of TOTAL 6500K->red reduction, the blue-elimination step (6500K->1900K) is the MAJORITY (~60-77%) and the green-removal step (1900K->pure red) is the MINORITY (~23-40%) — and these split figures are author-derived and modeling-sensitive, not literature-stated. Two findings further shrink the green payoff: St Hilaire 2022 (PNAS) shows that over a sustained multi-hour evening exposure the L+M-cone (green-relevant) channel contributes only ~7% of melatonin suppression (ipRGC 81%, S-cone 12%), with the green/cone contribution being largely TRANSIENT (dominant only in the first 1-3h; Gooley 2010 green sensitivity half-life ~38min). And the absolute melanopic level at 1900K is already deep in the diminishing-returns tail relative to the Brown 2022 consensus 10-lux / 1-lux evening ceilings. Conclusion: removing green below 1900K is a real-but-small marginal melanopic reduction that does not meaningfully change circadian stimulus for typical sustained evening use, while it collapses legibility (aviation 'red cockpit' literature: red markings invisible, color coding lost, near-point blur).

### Q2 — Optimal maximally-protective evening target

The practical maximally-protective evening target before diminishing returns and legibility collapse is a deep amber at ~1900K (the point where the engine's blue channel gain reaches zero). At/below ~1900K the spectral region carrying the dominant circadian stimulus (short-wavelength/blue, melanopic peak ~490nm, melatonin-suppression peak ~459-464nm) is fully eliminated; the melanopic-relevant FWHM band (~447-531nm) is essentially emptied, leaving only the green tail. The literature does NOT support pure red as a meaningfully more protective evening target for sustained screen use — long-wavelength red (>~620nm, s_mel <0.001 of peak) is circadian-SPARING but the increment from deep-amber to pure-red is the minority of the total melanopic reduction. The correct way to express the target is melanopic EDI, not Kelvin: the Brown 2022 (PLoS Biology) 18-author consensus sets <=10 lux melanopic EDI evening (>=3h before bed) and <=1 lux during sleep; CCT is explicitly NOT a valid proxy for biological potency (Esposito & Houser 2022, Sci Rep: mel-EDI varies up to ~2x at fixed CCT). The honest spectral-cutoff statement: eliminate blue (achieved at ~1900K) plus dim to get under the melanopic-EDI ceiling; chasing wavelengths beyond the ~531nm melanopic edge into pure red buys little additional circadian benefit while destroying readability. Individual sensitivity varies >50-fold (Phillips 2019, PNAS; group ED50 ~24.6 lux), so a single fixed CCT is not 'optimal' for everyone — argues for a user-adjustable slider with a sensible default, not a dogmatic pure-red maximum.

### Rationale (engine numbers)

Set Kelvin.warmestSupported = 1900 and EngineConfiguration.defaultWarmestPoint = Kelvin(1900) (down from the current Kelvin(500)). This is the hybrid of Option A (cap the everyday slider at 'blue 100% gone') with an evidence-honest justification rather than a hard ban on warmer values.

WHY 1900K AS THE FLOOR/DEFAULT WARMEST POINT:
1. It is the exact engine point where blue gain hits zero (verified in ColorTemperature.swift: blue=0 at temp<=19). 'Minimize blue light' is 100% achieved at 1900K; below it only green is removed, which is the minority of total melanopic reduction and largely transient over a sustained evening (St Hilaire 2022: L+M-cone ~7% of sustained suppression).
2. It fixes the compression problem the founder flagged. I computed the mired mapping in the actual engine: with warmestPoint=500, the everyday 3000-4000K range collapses into slider strength 0.05-0.10 (bottom ~10% of travel). With warmestPoint=1900, 4000K sits at strength ~0.26, 3000K at ~0.48, 2700K at ~0.58 — the comfortable range is spread across the usable middle of the slider.
3. It matches the de-facto industry 'warmest usable' default: f.lux's normal slider bottoms out at 1900K ('Candle') and gates redder values behind an explicit expanded range; Apple Night Shift caps warmer-limited (~2700K). 1900K is the industry-anchored 'maximally protective without breaking usability' point.
4. It preserves legibility. Below ~1900K toward pure red, the aviation/astronomy literature documents the legibility cliff (red markings invisible, color coding lost, near-point blur).

DEFAULT STRENGTH 0.7: with warmestPoint=1900, strength 0.7 maps to ~2412K (computed in-engine) — a warm, comfortable, clearly-warm-but-readable evening white in the f.lux 'Incandescent'-to-'Candle' band, well past neutral but not amber-extreme. (Note the onboarding view already uses warmestStrength=0.7 against a 1900K preview, so this aligns the shipped default with the onboarding preview.)

HYBRID ELEMENT (the Option B concession): if pure-red must remain reachable at all, gate it behind a SEPARATE explicitly-opt-in 'Maximum warmth / expanded range' advanced control (mirroring f.lux's wider-slider opt-in), NOT on the main everyday slider. The main slider should never bottom out at pure red. If product does not want the maintenance burden of a second control, pure Option A (floor at 1900K, no pure-red anywhere) is fully defensible and is my fallback recommendation — the evidence shows the sub-1900K increment is not worth the everyday-slider compression and legibility cost for the general-wellness use case.

FTC/§13 GUARDRAILS: frame as 'reaches zero blue-light emission at its warmest everyday setting' and 'supports healthy evening light habits', linking Brown 2022 / Schoellhorn 2023 rather than asserting sleep outcomes. Do NOT market 'lower Kelvin = more protective' as a guarantee (Esposito & Houser 2022: CCT is not a valid circadian proxy). Keep the circadian-SPARING (low melanopic stimulation) claim strictly separate from any red-light-therapy/photobiomodulation health claim (FTC Willow Curve $22M judgment, 2020).

### Uncertainties

1. QUANTITATIVE SPLIT IS MODELING-SENSITIVE. The headline numbers for 'what fraction of melanopic reduction the green step accounts for' are author-derived from a CIE-s_mel x sRGB-primary x Tanner-Helland model, NOT stated by any cited source. The audit of finding [59] could not reproduce the precise 26%/4%/77-23 figures: honest ranges are blue-step ~60-77%, green-step ~23-40%, and M/P at 1900K ~26-39% depending on the assumed green-primary peak (~530 vs ~550nm) and the green gain still being ~0.52 (half-on) at 1900K per the Tanner-Helland fit. Do not present a single precise percentage as established. The DIRECTION (blue step is the majority, green step the diminishing-returns minority) is robust; the exact magnitude is not.

2. PURE-RED ENDPOINT IS A MODEL EXTRAPOLATION. The ~505K 'green gain = 0 / pure red' point is below Tanner-Helland's nominally valid floor (~1000K); the green-fully-off endpoint is a model artifact, not an empirically grounded display state. This is another reason to not anchor product decisions on the sub-1900K region.

3. RED IS NOT UNCONDITIONALLY CIRCADIAN-INERT. Ho Mien 2014 showed high-irradiance 631nm red produced circadian PHASE shifts in some individuals via cones despite no melatonin suppression. 'Circadian-sparing' is intensity-dependent and relative, not absolute — but this strengthens, not weakens, the recommendation (pure red is not a magic circadian-zero state worth the legibility cost).

4. INDIVIDUAL VARIABILITY (>50-fold, Phillips 2019) means no single CCT/strength is correct for everyone; the slider default is a reasonable central choice, not an optimum. If product wants pure-red preserved for the most-sensitive tail of users, the hybrid advanced-dial path covers that without compressing the everyday slider.

5. DISPLAY-WARMING EFFICACY ON SLEEP OUTCOMES IS UNPROVEN. The Night Shift RCT (Duraccio 2021) found no measurable sleep benefit from partial blue reduction. The defensible claim is 'reduces blue-light exposure at night' (an input the engine demonstrably changes), never 'improves sleep'. The engine reaching zero blue at 1900K is exactly the input-side fact to market.

6. The recommendation assumes warmestSupported and defaultWarmestPoint are intended to move together to 1900K; if product wants to keep a deeper hard floor in the type while only changing the default/everyday cap, that is a UI/config decision the code cleanly supports (warmestPoint is per-state and published).

## Marketing evidence base (§13/FTC-safe)

### Headline claims

**1. [STRONG]** The light wavelengths that matter most for the body clock at night are in the blue (~459-490 nm). That is exactly the band Abendrot's warming removes first: blue output reaches zero at ~1900K.

> *§13-safe wording:* Research into the body's circadian system has identified short-wavelength blue light (peak sensitivity around 459-490 nm) as the dominant input for nighttime melatonin and circadian regulation [Brainard 2001; Thapan 2001; CIE S 026:2018]. Abendrot's warming attenuates the display's blue primary, reaching zero blue output at approximately 1900K.

- **Exact levels:** Melatonin-suppression action-spectrum peak: 464 nm (Brainard 2001, opsin-template fit R^2=0.91) and 459 nm (Thapan 2001, r^2=0.74). Most potent region 446-477 nm. Standardized melanopic (ipRGC) sensitivity peaks ~490 nm in vivo (CIE S 026:2018). Abendrot blue gain = 0 at ~1900K.
- **Cites:** Brainard et al. 2001, J Neurosci, DOI 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.21-16-06405.2001; Thapan et al. 2001, J Physiol, DOI 10.1111/j.1469-7793.2001.t01-1-00261.x; CIE S 026:2018, DOI 10.25039/S026.2018

**2. [STRONG]** It is the melanopic (short-wavelength) CONTENT of evening screen light, not the brightness or color appearance, that drives the effect on melatonin and sleep onset.

> *§13-safe wording:* In a controlled human display study, the melanopic (short-wavelength) content of evening screen light - independent of luminance and color appearance - was shown to determine its effect on melatonin and time to fall asleep [Schoellhorn et al. 2023]. Abendrot is designed to lower a screen's melanopic content by attenuating the blue primary.

- **Exact levels:** n=72 healthy males (18-35y); exposure ~4 h before habitual bedtime; melanopic EDI manipulated independent of luminance/color via 5-primary metameric display (~200-300% melanopsin Weber contrast). Low-melanopic (~5 lx mEDI) vs high-melanopic (~150 lx mEDI): ~50% shorter mean sleep latency and attenuated melatonin suppression under low-melanopic.
- **Cites:** Schoellhorn et al. 2023, Communications Biology, DOI 10.1038/s42003-023-04598-4; Gimenez et al. 2022, J Pineal Res, DOI 10.1111/jpi.12786

**3. [MODERATE]** Long-wavelength red light is circadian-sparing: at practical evening intensities it produces minimal-to-no melatonin suppression, while matched blue light suppresses melatonin.

> *§13-safe wording:* Research shows that long-wavelength (red) light is circadian-sparing - it produces little to no nocturnal melatonin suppression at practical evening intensities, whereas matched blue light does [Figueiro & Rea 2010; Sanchez-Cano et al. 2025]. This is a low-melanopic-stimulation property and is distinct from any 'red-light therapy' health claim.

- **Exact levels:** Figueiro & Rea 2010: red 625 nm vs blue 470 nm, both 40 lux at the cornea, 1 h at night; only blue significantly suppressed melatonin (blue vs dark P=.006, blue vs red P=.004); red was statistically indistinguishable from darkness. Sanchez-Cano 2025: red 631 nm vs blue 464 nm, 3 h (21:00-24:00), 80 lux photopic, n=12; at 2 h melatonin 26.0 pg/mL (red) vs 7.5 pg/mL (blue), p=0.019; at 3 h 16.6 vs 8.3 pg/mL, p=0.013.
- **Cites:** Figueiro & Rea 2010, Int J Endocrinol, DOI 10.1155/2010/829351; Sanchez-Cano et al. 2025, Life (MDPI), DOI 10.3390/life15050715

**4. [STRONG]** Eliminating blue (the ~1900K point) removes the dominant circadian stimulus; per the CIE melanopic curve, the residual green region carries far less weight and red is essentially off the active range.

> *§13-safe wording:* Per the official CIE S 026:2018 melanopic action spectrum, sensitivity falls steeply beyond green and is near-zero in the red. Once the blue primary is removed (Abendrot reaches zero blue at ~1900K), the dominant melanopic stimulus is already eliminated; the green region carries a much smaller weight and red is effectively outside the active band.

- **Exact levels:** CIE S 026:2018 melanopic relative sensitivity (peak=1.0 at 490 nm): 460nm=0.708, 480nm=0.966, 490nm=1.000, 500nm=0.966, 530nm=0.519, 550nm=0.216, 555nm=0.162, 580nm=0.0269, 600nm=0.00507 (~0.5% of peak), 620nm=0.000947 (~0.1%), 650nm=0.0000866 (~0.009%). sRGB green primary (~550 nm) carries ~0.22 weight; red primary (~610 nm) ~0.002.
- **Cites:** CIE S 026:2018, DOI 10.25039/S026.2018 (official data file S026_Table2_Data.xlsx); Schlangen & Price 2021, Front Neurol, DOI 10.3389/fneur.2021.624861

**5. [STRONG]** Expert consensus gives concrete evening/night light targets in melanopic EDI: <=10 lux in the evening (>=3 h before bed) and <=1 lux during sleep, vs >=250 lux in the daytime.

> *§13-safe wording:* An 18-author international expert consensus recommends keeping evening melanopic EDI at or below 10 lux (starting at least 3 hours before bed) and the sleep environment below 1 lux, versus a daytime minimum of 250 lux [Brown et al. 2022]. These are healthy-adult reference targets - not medical thresholds. Abendrot helps users lower the melanopic content of on-screen light toward these reference levels.

- **Exact levels:** Daytime minimum 250 lux melanopic EDI; evening maximum 10 lux melanopic EDI (starting >=3 h before bedtime); sleep environment maximum 1 lux melanopic EDI (10 lux if vision is needed at night). All measured at the eye, vertical plane, ~1.2 m height. mEDI defined per CIE S 026:2018 (K_mel,v(D65) = 1.3262 mW/lm).
- **Cites:** Brown et al. 2022, PLoS Biology, DOI 10.1371/journal.pbio.3001571; CIE S 026:2018, DOI 10.25039/S026.2018

**6. [STRONG]** Correlated color temperature (Kelvin) is NOT a reliable proxy for circadian potency; at one fixed CCT melanopic EDI can vary roughly two-fold by spectral composition - so 'lower Kelvin = more protective' must not be stated as a guarantee.

> *§13-safe wording:* Research shows correlated color temperature (CCT) is not a suitable proxy for the biological potency of light: at a fixed CCT, melanopic EDI can vary roughly two-fold depending on spectral composition [Esposito & Houser 2022]. The relevant quantity is melanopic content (where blue is removed), not the Kelvin label itself.

- **Exact levels:** At a fixed 5000 K (300 lx photopic, Rf>=70), melanopic EDI varied from 196 to 319 lx (a 123 lx spread). Across 2500-6000 K, achievable melanopic EDI ranged 123-354 lx at matched illuminance/fidelity.
- **Cites:** Esposito & Houser 2022, Scientific Reports, DOI 10.1038/s41598-022-21755-7

**7. [STRONG]** Individual sensitivity to evening light varies more than 50-fold, so no single warmth/brightness setting is right for everyone - which is why Abendrot offers an adjustable slider and uses humble, non-guarantee language.

> *§13-safe wording:* Human sensitivity to evening light varies more than 50-fold between individuals [Phillips et al. 2019], so no single setting is optimal for everyone. Abendrot provides a user-adjustable warmth slider and frames its effect as variable individual support, never a guaranteed outcome.

- **Exact levels:** Group-level ED50 for melatonin suppression = 24.6 lux; individual ED50 ranged 6 lux to 350 lux (>50-fold). 50% melatonin suppression occurred at <30 lux at the group level (comparable to typical indoor/device light). n=55, ages 18-30.
- **Cites:** Phillips et al. 2019, PNAS, DOI 10.1073/pnas.1901824116

**8. [STRONG]** Most of the circadian benefit comes from leaving the bright/blue-rich zone (the steep part of the dose-response), since even dim-to-moderate light suppresses much melatonin and the response saturates near ~200 lux.

> *§13-safe wording:* Human melatonin suppression follows a steep, saturating dose-response to light: half-maximal suppression occurs at only ~50-130 lux and the response approaches saturation near ~200 lux [Zeitzer et al. 2000]. The largest reductions therefore come from getting out of the bright/blue-rich zone rather than from chasing the last few percent toward pure red.

- **Exact levels:** Half-maximal melatonin suppression at ~50-130 lux (4-parameter logistic ED50 = 106 +/- 13.3 lux); near-saturation by ~200 lux; phase-resetting half-maximal ~80-160 lux, saturating ~550 lux; ~1% of 9000 lux bright light (~100 lux room light) yields half the maximal response. n=23, 6.5 h nocturnal exposure.
- **Cites:** Zeitzer et al. 2000, J Physiol, DOI 10.1111/j.1469-7793.2000.00695.x

**9. [STRONG]** Green's circadian weight is real but largely transient: cones (including the ~550 nm green region) contribute mainly in the first 1-3 hours of an exposure, after which melanopsin (blue, ~481 nm) dominates over a sustained evening.

> *§13-safe wording:* The circadian action spectrum is duration-dependent: over a sustained multi-hour evening exposure, melanopsin in the blue (~481-485 nm) dominates, with the green-region cone contribution being strong mainly early and then collapsing [St Hilaire et al. 2022; Gooley et al. 2010]. Over a full evening the long-duration blue channel - which Abendrot zeroes by ~1900K - is the dominant driver.

- **Exact levels:** St Hilaire 2022 (6.5 h): whole-exposure melatonin-suppression lambda-max = 481 nm (lens-corrected), phase-resetting 483 nm; first quarter peak 441 nm with secondary 550 nm (S-cone 51%, L+M-cone 47%); final quarter 485 nm. Three-channel split of full response: ipRGC/melanopsin 81%, S-cone 12%, L+M-cone 7%. Gooley 2010: 555 nm green sensitivity decayed relative to 460 nm with a half-life of ~38 min.
- **Cites:** St Hilaire et al. 2022, PNAS, DOI 10.1073/pnas.2205301119; Gooley et al. 2010, Sci Transl Med, DOI 10.1126/scitranslmed.3000741

**10. [MODERATE]** Removing blue at the source (the protective variable) - not the color 'red' per se - is what blocks light-induced melatonin suppression, even under bright light.

> *§13-safe wording:* Blocking the blue band alone is sufficient to prevent light-induced melatonin suppression in humans, even under bright light, establishing that the protective variable is the removal of short-wavelength content rather than the color red itself [Sasseville et al. 2006].

- **Exact levels:** 60-min bright-light pulse, 1300 lux behind filters, 01:00-02:00. Orange (blue-blocking) lens: non-significant +6% melatonin change (no suppression); grey control lens: significant -46% melatonin suppression (P<0.05). n=14.
- **Cites:** Sasseville et al. 2006, J Pineal Res, DOI 10.1111/j.1600-079X.2006.00332.x

**11. [MODERATE]** Software blue-reduction modes have not shown a measurable sleep benefit in randomized real-world testing, so the honest claim is 'reduces blue-light exposure at night', not 'improves sleep'.

> *§13-safe wording:* A randomized real-world study found no measurable sleep-outcome difference from a software blue-reduction mode (Apple Night Shift) versus no reduction [Duraccio et al. 2021]. Accordingly, Abendrot describes what it demonstrably does - reduces blue-light exposure at night - rather than promising a sleep improvement.

- **Exact levels:** Randomized, n=167 emerging adults (18-24, 71.3% female), 7 consecutive nights, wrist accelerometry: no significant difference in sleep outcomes between iPhone-with-Night-Shift, iPhone-without-Night-Shift, and no-phone groups (the full-sample result was null).
- **Cites:** Duraccio, Zaugg, Blackburn, Jensen 2021, Sleep Health, DOI 10.1016/j.sleh.2021.03.005

**12. [STRONG]** ipRGCs are a distinct third class of retinal photoreceptor that set the circadian clock, and their melanopsin pigment is maximally sensitive to blue light (~480-484 nm) - the physiological reason blue is the circadian lever.

> *§13-safe wording:* Melanopsin-containing ipRGCs are a distinct class of retinal photoreceptor that drive the circadian clock, with intrinsic peak sensitivity in the blue at ~480-484 nm [Berson et al. 2002; Provencio et al. 2000; Bailes & Lucas 2013]. This is the physiological basis for blue being the key circadian wavelength.

- **Exact levels:** Intrinsic ipRGC action-spectrum lambda-max = 484 nm (Berson 2002); human melanopsin photopigment lambda-max ~479 nm (Bailes & Lucas 2013). Human OPN4 gene: 10 exons, chromosome 10q22, 2.3-kb cDNA; expression restricted to inner-retinal ganglion/amacrine cells, absent from rod/cone photoreceptors (Provencio 2000).
- **Cites:** Berson, Dunn & Takao 2002, Science, DOI 10.1126/science.1067262; Provencio et al. 2000, J Neurosci, DOI 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.20-02-00600.2000; Bailes & Lucas 2013, Proc R Soc B, DOI 10.1098/rspb.2012.2987

### Red-light story (circadian-sparing, NOT therapy)

Abendrot's red/warm evening story is a CIRCADIAN-SPARING story, not a therapy story. The science is about what red light does NOT do to the body clock.

The body's circadian system is driven mainly by a dedicated blue-light sensor. Melanopsin-containing ipRGCs - a distinct third class of retinal photoreceptor that sets the circadian clock (Berson 2002; Provencio 2000) - are maximally sensitive to blue light around 480 nm (Bailes & Lucas 2013, human melanopsin lambda-max ~479 nm). The two foundational human action spectra for melatonin suppression both land in the blue: 464 nm (Brainard 2001) and 459 nm (Thapan 2001), with the most potent region being 446-477 nm. The standardized CIE S 026:2018 melanopic curve peaks at 490 nm and falls steeply toward longer wavelengths - by 600 nm it is ~0.5% of peak, by 620 nm ~0.1%.

Because long-wavelength red light sits on the far, near-zero tail of that curve, it is circadian-sparing: it stimulates the melanopic system very little. This is shown directly in humans. Figueiro & Rea (2010) exposed people to red (625 nm) and blue (470 nm) at the SAME 40 lux at the eye for an hour at night - only blue suppressed melatonin; red was statistically indistinguishable from darkness. Sanchez-Cano et al. (2025) compared red 631 nm vs blue 464 nm over a 3-hour evening exposure: under red, melatonin recovered to 26.0 pg/mL while blue held it suppressed at 7.5 pg/mL (p=0.019). And the protective variable is the short-wavelength removal itself, not the color red: blocking just the blue band with orange lenses prevented melatonin suppression under bright light, while neutral-grey lenses did not (Sasseville 2006).

Two honesty guardrails are built into this story. First, circadian-sparing is RELATIVE, not absolute: at extreme irradiance, narrow-band red (631 nm at ~13 log photons/cm^2/s) still produced cone-mediated circadian phase shifts in some individuals even without melatonin suppression (Ho Mien/Gooley 2014). So we say 'low-stimulus / circadian-sparing', never 'zero circadian effect'. Second, and most important: this is the low-melanopic-stimulation claim ONLY. It is categorically different from 'red-light therapy' / photobiomodulation health claims (treating pain, healing tissue, 'clinically proven'), which are a separate, FTC-enforced, far weaker evidence base - the FTC won a $22M judgment against one such device for unsupported 'clinically proven' claims (FTC 2020). Abendrot must never conflate the two. Our claim is simply: warm/red evening light puts far less energy in the blue band the body clock is tuned to detect.

Engineering footnote we can stand behind: once Abendrot's warming zeroes the blue primary (~1900K), the dominant melanopic stimulus is already removed; pushing further toward pure red removes only the residual green channel, a real but small additional melanopic reduction (green ~0.22 vs blue's much larger weight on the CIE curve), while legibility degrades - which is why mainstream warming tools like f.lux set their normal floor at ~1900K and gate redder values behind an opt-in advanced range.

### DO NOT CLAIM (overclaim / FTC guardrails)

- Do NOT make red-light-therapy / photobiomodulation health claims (treats pain, heals tissue, reduces inflammation, boosts recovery/cognition). Abendrot's claim is ONLY 'red/warm light is circadian-sparing / low-melanopic'. These are categorically different evidence bases; conflating them is the single biggest §13/FTC risk (FTC won a $22M judgment over 'clinically proven' LLLT pain claims).
- Do NOT say 'clinically proven', 'proven to improve sleep', 'cures insomnia', or 'treats insomnia'. Abendrot is a general-wellness app, not a medical device. Software blue-reduction has NOT shown measurable sleep benefit in randomized testing (Duraccio 2021).
- Do NOT guarantee 'improves sleep' or 'you will fall asleep faster' as a promised outcome. The honest claim is 'reduces blue-light exposure at night' / 'supports healthy evening light habits' - an input the engine demonstrably changes, not a guaranteed downstream result.
- Do NOT say 'blue light damages your eyes' or imply retinal/eye-damage protection. The evidence base here is circadian (melatonin/sleep timing), not ocular damage; that is a different and unsupported claim.
- Do NOT claim 'cures eye strain' or 'prevents eye strain'. Not supported by the cited circadian literature.
- Do NOT assert 'lower Kelvin = more circadian-protective' as a guarantee. CCT is not a valid circadian proxy - melanopic EDI varies ~2-fold at a fixed CCT (Esposito & Houser 2022). Speak in melanopic terms.
- Do NOT claim red light has 'zero circadian effect' or is 'circadian-inert'. It is circadian-SPARING / low-stimulus and this is RELATIVE and intensity-dependent - high-irradiance red still produced cone-mediated phase shifts in some people (Ho Mien/Gooley 2014).
- Do NOT claim pure red is necessary or universally optimal. Individual sensitivity varies >50-fold (Phillips 2019); the consensus evening target (<=10 lux mEDI, Brown 2022) is reached well before pure red, and green's circadian weight is largely transient (St Hilaire 2022).
- Do NOT cite the Sanchez-Cano 2025 red-vs-blue pg/mL values (26.0 vs 7.5) as coming from Ho Mien/Gooley 2014 - that PLOS ONE study has NO blue arm and reports % AUC, not pg/mL. Keep the two sources separate.
- Do NOT overstate Brainard 2001 fit quality as 'r=0.81-0.99'; the correct fluence-response range is r=0.81-0.95.
- Do NOT present the derived display M/P figures (e.g., '77/23 split', 'M/P 26% at 1900K', '4% at pure red') as established/peer-reviewed; they are self-derived model estimates with wide ranges - present only as illustrative engineering modeling, not as cited fact.
- Do NOT attribute the f.lux '1900K floor / no biological reason to go lower' rationale to f.lux's makers; it is unofficial forum (community user) opinion. Cite only the verifiable product behavior (1900K default floor, opt-in wider range) to the official f.lux FAQ.
- Do NOT cite the Duraccio 2021 Night Shift study as evidence for the 1900K/melanopic-threshold claim - it tested only Night Shift on/off/no-phone and says nothing about CCT targets or melanopic EDI.
- Do NOT present Brown et al. 2022 consensus numbers as regulatory or medical limits - they are healthy-adult, group-average reference targets with explicit individual-variability caveats; frame as 'reference targets', never promised outcomes.
- Do NOT misattribute the melanopic ~490 nm peak / mEDI definition to 'Spitschan et al.' for PMC7970181 - the correct authors are Schlangen & Price 2021 (Front Neurol, DOI 10.3389/fneur.2021.624861).

## Skeptic critic — must-fix before launch

**Verdict:** SOUND AND ACTIONABLE AS AN ENGINEERING DECISION; CONDITIONALLY SAFE AS A MARKETING BASE, with the red-light story and the brightness omission as the two things that must be fixed before launch. ENGINEERING SIDE: I independently verified every load-bearing code/numeric claim against the actual source. Blue gain = 0 at <=1900K is real (ColorTemperature.swift:89-90; gain(1900K).blue=0.000). Green is still ~0.519 ('half-on') at 1900K - confirmed and notable. The mired-compression argument is real and I reproduced it exactly: with the current shipped warmestPoint=500, the everyday 3000-4000K band collapses to strength 0.05-0.10, and - a point the memo UNDERSELLS - the shipped default (strength 0.7 @ warmest=500) currently resolves to ~691K, i.e. near-pure red as the everyday default. That alone justifies the recommended change to warmestPoint=1900 (where 0.7 -> 2412K, also verified). The hybrid recommendation (floor/default at 1900K, gate pure-red behind an opt-in advanced dial) is evidence-honest and the fallback (pure Option A) is defensible. MARKETING SIDE: the citation library is unusually rigorous and self-correcting (it flags its own fabricated bylines, forbids the therapy conflation, cites the NULL Duraccio RCT against its own interest, and bans the unreproducible M/P percentages). That honesty is the dossier's biggest strength. BUT three gaps keep it from 'launch-ready': (1) the red-light hero rests on the model's least-valid sub-1900K region, which the memo itself calls an extrapolation; (2) there is NO bridge from 'zero blue gain' to actual melanopic EDI at the eye, and BRIGHTNESS - the dominant dose variable per the company's own Zeitzer 2000 citation - is uncontrolled and undisclosed; (3) the 'illustrative vs cited' firewall around precise s_mel/M-P numbers is procedurally thin and a designer will likely breach it in a graphic. NET: ship the 1900K engine change with confidence. For marketing, lead with the verifiable input fact ('reaches zero blue-light EMISSION at its warmest everyday setting' + 'pair with lower brightness'), keep red as a circadian-SPARING footnote not a hero, never juxtapose the product sentence with sleep-latency data, and add an IMAGERY guardrail (not just wording) against implied red-light-therapy. With those fixes it is safe; as written it carries a moderate net-impression risk on therapy-conflation and sleep-benefit-by-juxtaposition.

### Gaps
- NO OCULAR / EYE-STRAIN ANGLE RESEARCHED, BUT THE PRODUCT NAME AND CATEGORY INVITE IT. The dossier correctly bans 'blue light damages your eyes' and 'cures eye strain' in doNotClaim, but it does NOT research or provide safe substitute wording for the digital-eye-strain / visual-comfort claim that a warmth app will almost inevitably want to make. This is a gap: the most commercially tempting adjacent claim is left with a 'do not' and no vetted alternative, so marketing will improvise unsafe wording. Recommend adding a researched 'visual comfort / reduced glare at night' lane with its own evidence floor.
- MELANOPIC-EDI BRIDGE FROM GAIN TO BIOLOGY IS NEVER QUANTIFIED FOR ABENDROT ITSELF. Every claim about the engine stops at 'attenuates the blue primary / zero blue at 1900K' (an input fact, verified in ColorTemperature.swift). But the entire Brown 2022 story is denominated in melanopic EDI at the eye, which depends on absolute display luminance, ambient light, viewing distance, and screen content - none of which the gamma/overlay engine controls or measures. There is no analysis connecting 'blue gain = 0' to any actual mEDI delta at the eye. So the marketing juxtaposes a hard engine fact (zero blue) with a consensus lux target (<=10 lux mEDI) and lets the reader infer Abendrot gets them there - which it cannot guarantee, because a bright white screen at 1900K can still exceed 10 lux mEDI. This is the biggest unresearched confound: BRIGHTNESS. Zeitzer 2000 (cited) shows the dose-response is driven by intensity, and the engine does not dim. The memo even concedes '1900K is already deep in the diminishing-returns tail relative to the 10-lux ceiling' but never reconciles that the app may sit ABOVE the ceiling at full brightness.
- NO VERIFICATION THAT THE OVERLAY/GAMMA PATH ACTUALLY DELIVERS THE TANNER-HELLAND GAINS ON A REAL PANEL. The whole spectral argument assumes the computed RGBGain maps to a real reduction in emitted blue radiance. rgbGain() is a normalized multiply veil / DDC gain; on an emissive LCD/OLED with a specific blue-primary SPD, a 'blue=0 gain' multiply does not necessarily zero blue PHOTON output the same way across panels, and the sRGB-primary-peak assumption (~465nm blue, ~549nm green) is itself an approximation. The memo's own uncertainty #2 flags the sub-1900K region as a model artifact, but the 'zero blue at 1900K' marketing headline rests on the same model and is presented as hard fact. No measured-radiance validation exists in-repo.
- SCREEN-LIGHT vs WHITE-LIGHT EVIDENCE MISMATCH IS UNADDRESSED. Most cited human studies (Figueiro 2010, Sanchez-Cano 2025, Sasseville 2006, Zeitzer 2000, Phillips 2019) used narrowband LEDs or full-field bright light at the cornea, NOT a warmed self-luminous display at arm's length. Only Schoellhorn 2023 and Chang 2015 are actual display studies, and Duraccio 2021 (the one real-world software-mode RCT) was NULL. The dossier leans on the strong narrowband studies to carry a display claim. This external-validity gap is the substantive scientific weak point and is not called out as a limitation in the marketing-facing wording.
- NO RESEARCH ON THE 'GREEN CAST / COLOR FIDELITY' UX COUNTERARGUMENT TO THE 1900K DEFAULT. The memo recommends defaulting to ~2412K (strength 0.7) and capping at 1900K, but provides no analysis of whether a green gain of 0.52 at 1900K (verified) produces an acceptable on-screen white or an unpleasant muddy/amber cast for sustained reading. The legibility argument is borrowed wholesale from aviation 'red cockpit' literature, which is about pure red, not 1900-2400K amber - so the cited legibility evidence is for a different regime than the recommended setting.

The memo itself concedes (uncertainties #1, #2) that the sub-1900K / pure-red endpoint is a MODEL EXTRAPOLATION below Tanner-Helland's valid floor (~1000K), and that the 'green step is the minority' split is author-derived, not literature-stated, with wide ranges (blue 60-77% / green 23-40%). Yet the marketing redLightStory and spectrumTable present a confident '~505K = pure red' endpoint and precise s_mel weights as if grounded. If marketing leans into 'red/warm' as the hero, it is anchoring on the weakest-evidenced part of the entire analysis.
- 'CIRCADIAN-SPARING' IS RELATIVE AND INTENSITY-DEPENDENT, AND THE MEMO KNOWS IT (uncertainty #3, Ho Mien 2014: high-irradiance 631nm red still caused cone-mediated phase shifts with no melatonin suppression). The marketing wording is mostly careful ('low-melanopic-stimulation', 'circadian-sparing, never zero'), but the redLightStory narrative momentum ('only blue suppressed melatonin; red was statistically indistinguishable from darkness') invites a reader to hear 'red = safe/inert'. The guardrail is one sentence against several persuasive sentences. Imbalanced.
- THE SANCHEZ-CANO 2025 EVIDENCE IS A WEAK PILLAR DOING VISIBLE WORK. - SELF-DERIVED DISPLAY M/P FIGURES. The memo's headline quantitative split (26-39% mel output at 1900K, 77/23 blue-vs-green) could not be reproduced even by the memo's own audit (uncertainty #1). ### Overclaim risks
- RED-LIGHT-THERAPY CONFLATION IS THE PRIMARY §13 RISK AND THE DOSSIER CORRECTLY IDENTIFIES IT (FTC Willow Curve $22M judgment, 2020). The doNotClaim list is well-constructed. RESIDUAL RISK: the product story is literally a 'red/warm evening' story with a hero red narrative; the brand name Abendrot ('sunset red') plus heavy red-spectrum imagery creates IMPLIED-therapy risk even if no words cross the line. FTC reads net impression, not just literal claims. A red-glowing 'wellness/sleep' app is one bad hero headline ('red light for better nights') away from being read as a therapy claim. Recommend an explicit visual/imagery guardrail, not just a wording guardrail.
- 'SUPPORTS HEALTHY EVENING LIGHT HABITS' + SLEEP-ADJACENT IMAGERY = IMPLIED SLEEP-IMPROVEMENT CLAIM. The dossier bans 'improves sleep' as an outcome (good) and cites the NULL Duraccio 2021 software-mode RCT (excellent honesty). But several s13SafeWording strings pair 'evening', 'melatonin', 'sleep latency' (Schoellhorn) with the product in the same sentence. Even with hedged verbs, juxtaposing the product with melatonin/sleep-latency findings can create the net impression of a sleep benefit the company's own cited RCT failed to find. The Schoellhorn claim wording 'Abendrot is designed to lower a screen's melanopic content' sits right after sleep-latency data - net impression risk.
- CCT-AS-PROXY GUARANTEE: the dossier explicitly forbids 'lower Kelvin = more protective' (Esposito & Houser 2022) - well handled. Residual risk: any marketing graphic that shows a Kelvin slider with a 'more protective ->' arrow re-introduces exactly the forbidden proxy claim visually. Same firewall-is-thin problem as the M/P figures.
- BRIGHTNESS OMISSION CREATES AN OVERCLAIM BY OMISSION. Marketing that says 'reaches zero blue at its warmest setting' while implying circadian benefit, without disclosing that screen BRIGHTNESS (which the app does not control) is the dominant dose variable per the company's own cited Zeitzer 2000, risks a misleading net impression that warmth alone meaningfully reduces circadian load. The honest claim must couple warmth with 'and dim your screen', or it overstates the engine's contribution.
- PUBLISHING PRECISE-LOOKING NUMBERS (s_mel weights to 4 decimals, 'blue carries ~0.785, red ~0.002') lends a false air of measured precision to what are model-derived sRGB-primary approximations. Stated as fact in marketing, these invite a substantiation demand the company cannot fully meet for its specific panels. Keep them clearly labelled as standard-curve illustrations, never as 'Abendrot reduces your melanopic exposure by X%'.
